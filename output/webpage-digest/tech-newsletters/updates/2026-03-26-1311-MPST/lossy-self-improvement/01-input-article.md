# Lossy Self-Improvement

By Nathan Lambert | March 22, 2026

## Overview

While rapid AI progress is real, Nathan Lambert argues against the "fast takeoff" narrative. Instead of recursive self-improvement (RSI) leading to exponential intelligence growth, Lambert proposes **lossy self-improvement** (LSI)--where friction and inefficiencies prevent true exponential acceleration.

## The Current Moment

AI models are genuinely transforming work. Two or three labs dominate with superior resources. Language models excel at code and CLI-based tasks. These changes create momentum suggesting continued acceleration.

However, Lambert contends the trend will appear "more linear than exponential when we reflect back."

## Three Core Frictions

### 1. Automatable Research Is Too Narrow

While AI tools optimize specific metrics (like test loss), meaningful research requires balancing multiple objectives simultaneously. "The most provocative case is for pretraining...we don't know if that'll be economically more valuable."

Current benchmarks measuring AI research capability suffer from narrow scope. Post-training remains complex--"over 90% of the challenge...is getting the last 1-3% of performance."

The gap between mathematical accuracy and practical usefulness persists. Human intuition managing complexity still outpaces automated optimization.

### 2. Diminishing Returns from Parallel Agents

**Amdahl's law** applies: parallelization hits saturation limits. Adding thousands of AI agents won't proportionally increase research output when human supervision becomes the bottleneck.

One researcher using AI assistance gains massive productivity. Ten agents supporting one human? Diminishing returns. Hundreds of agents? Few humans can effectively coordinate that many tasks daily.

### 3. Resource Bottlenecks and Politics

Organizational constraints matter. Research leadership controls resource allocation. Billions in compute spending won't remain isolated for pure AI self-improvement experimentation.

Political decisions about resource distribution impose friction that automation cannot remove.

## The Actual Trajectory

Engineering automation is real today. Basic model optimization increasingly involves AI. But a third phase--where AI creates fundamental breakthroughs--faces steeper challenges than incremental improvements.

Models excel at "hill climbing" but struggle producing paradigm shifts needed for continual learning, world models, or post-scaling innovations.

## Conclusion

AI is becoming central to its own development loop. This warrants excitement and concern. Yet sustained linear progress differs fundamentally from exponential takeoff. The industry will remain in "lossy self-improvement for a few years," but this insufficient for singularity-style acceleration.
